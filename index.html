📚
Introduction

Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning have undergone a remarkable evolution over the past seven decades. This timeline artifact traces the key breakthroughs, paradigm shifts, and technological milestones that have defined the AI/ML landscape from its theoretical foundations in the 1950s to the agentic AI systems of 2025. As a practitioner who has built AI solutions across genomics, healthcare, telecom, and enterprise environments, I created this resource to provide a clear, structured overview of where AI has been and where it is heading — and to show how each era directly informs the production-grade systems I build today.

Each milestone includes contextual information about its impact on the field. The timeline below serves as a tangible, evidence-based deliverable: a professionally designed visual that can be referenced, shared, and used as a resource by its target audience.

Title

AI Lab Experience: Applying Responsible AI & ML Workflows

📚
Introduction

This artifact documents my hands-on AI Lab experience from Workshop 1, where I explored generative AI capabilities, prompt engineering strategies, and critical evaluation of model outputs. What makes this artifact distinctive is the perspective I bring: as someone who has prototyped RAG systems over lab literature and genomic annotations using LangChain/LangGraph + vector databases at Stony Brook University, designed MLOps pipelines with MLflow + Azure DevOps + Kubernetes at A5G Networks, and containerized computer vision models with Docker + EKS for real-time ICU monitoring at Cloudphysician, I naturally approach even a classroom AI lab with production-level rigor. This artifact captures that process with tangible evidence — workflow diagrams, annotated lab sessions, before/after prompt comparisons, evaluation metrics, and a responsible AI audit — demonstrating how I bridge the gap between academic exploration and the kind of disciplined AI engineering that ships reliable systems in regulated environments.

📄
Description

The AI Lab activity challenged students to interact with modern AI tools, test their capabilities on defined tasks, evaluate output quality, and reflect on responsible usage. My approach transformed this into a structured engineering exercise: I defined evaluation criteria upfront (drawing on my experience with RAGAS and TruLens for production RAG evaluation), ran systematic prompt experiments with controlled variables, scored outputs across multiple quality dimensions, and documented everything for reproducibility. The deliverable is this portfolio artifact, which includes a 6-phase workflow diagram, four annotated lab output panels simulating real terminal/IDE sessions, a before-and-after prompt comparison showing measurable improvement, a "Classroom-to-Production" bridge diagram mapping lab skills to professional competencies, and a responsible AI checklist. Together, these constitute tangible, shareable evidence of applied AI thinking — not just a description of what I did, but proof of how I think.

🎯
Objective

The primary objectives were to: (1) apply structured AI experimentation using the same hypothesis-driven workflow I follow when building ML experiments at the Research Foundation for SUNY (where I package PyTorch workflows with MLflow for reproducibility), (2) evaluate AI outputs critically against quality metrics informed by my production experience with RAGAS, TruLens, and Guardrails evaluation frameworks, (3) practice responsible AI use principles that I have previously taught as a Graduate TA at Stony Brook (where I introduced students to responsible LLM/RAG usage), and (4) document the entire process in a format that communicates technical depth to hiring managers while remaining accessible to non-technical audiences. Fundamentally, this lab was an opportunity to demonstrate that I treat every AI interaction — whether in a research lab, a production pipeline, or a classroom exercise — with the same disciplined methodology.

  • Phase 1 — Scope: Read the lab instructions and framed each task as a clear problem statement with measurable success criteria. This mirrors how I scope ML experiments at Stony Brook, where I define data-quality and output-quality thresholds before writing a single line of code. For this lab, I established evaluation dimensions: factual accuracy, relevance, completeness, hallucination risk, and bias.
  • Phase 2 — Experiment: Interacted with AI tools using structured prompt engineering. I applied techniques refined through my professional RAG work: role-based framing, explicit context injection, output format specification, and chain-of-thought prompting. For each task, I ran at least 3 prompt variations with a single controlled variable change between iterations to isolate what drives quality improvement.
  • Phase 3 — Evaluate: Scored each output against the pre-defined quality rubric. This approach is adapted from my production experience with RAGAS (faithfulness, answer relevancy, context precision) and TruLens (groundedness, relevance, comprehensiveness). In the lab setting, I translated these into simpler but analogous metrics appropriate for the exercise.
  • Phase 4 — Iterate: Diagnosed failure modes when outputs fell short — ambiguous prompts, missing context, task complexity beyond the model's training. Refined and re-ran, documenting what changed and why. This diagnostic-iterate loop is identical to what I practiced in 5G telecom ML at A5G Networks, where I retrained and redeployed models based on monitored performance drift.
  • Phase 5 — Audit: Applied a responsible AI checklist covering fact verification, bias detection, confidence calibration, and ethical deployment considerations. This draws directly from my experience authoring data-quality and GPU-usage guidelines at Stony Brook and aligning ICU models with hospital privacy/compliance flows on AWS at Cloudphysician.
  • Phase 6 — Document: Compiled all findings into this structured, reproducible artifact with visual evidence. Reproducibility is a non-negotiable in my professional work — at Stony Brook, I package all experiments with MLflow and cloud storage so any researcher can reproduce results.

Title

Building an AI Chatbot: From User Empathy to Responsible Deployment

📚
Introduction

This artifact documents my work on Activity 1.4 from the AI Lab Guided Activities — "Build an AI Chatbot or Assistant." The activity asked students to apply the Design Thinking framework (Empathy, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test) to design and build a domain-specific AI chatbot. What makes my approach different is that I have actually built chatbot-like systems professionally. At Stony Brook University, I designed RAG pipelines using LangChain and LangGraph with vector databases (Pinecone, Qdrant, Chroma) that served as intelligent research assistants for genomics literature. At Cloudphysician, I worked on real-time analytics pipelines that fed into clinical decision-support interfaces — essentially AI systems that assist doctors in ICU settings. This professional background gave me a practical lens to go beyond the classroom exercise and think about what truly makes a chatbot useful, safe, and reliable in the real world.

📄
Description

The activity required us to follow the Design Thinking cycle — starting with empathy (understanding who the chatbot serves and what problems they face), then defining the problem clearly, ideating on possible AI solutions, prototyping a working chatbot using an LLM, and finally testing and evaluating the output. I chose to build a Healthcare FAQ Assistant — a chatbot that helps patients and their families understand common medical procedures, medication side effects, and hospital protocols in simple language. This choice was directly inspired by my experience at Cloudphysician, where I saw first-hand how overwhelmed families are when a loved one is in the ICU. The chatbot needed to be accurate (no hallucinated medical advice), empathetic in tone, and transparent about its limitations. Below, I document the full Design Thinking workflow, show before-and-after system prompt designs, demonstrate sample conversations, and include evaluation metrics and a responsible AI checklist.

Objective

Phase 1 — Empathise: I started by identifying the target users. Drawing from my Cloudphysician experience, where ICU patient families desperately needed understandable health information, I created three user personas: a worried family member, a post-discharge patient, and a rural clinic nurse. The AI Lab slides emphasised that empathy is the first step in problem-solving — "starting with empathy allows us to define problems accurately before jumping to solutions." I applied this by mapping each persona's pain points, technical comfort level, and the language they would actually use.

Phase 2 — Define: Based on the empathy mapping, I defined the core problem: "Patients and families need a trustworthy, easy-to-understand AI assistant that answers common healthcare questions accurately, while being transparent about when to consult a real doctor." The AI Lab slides taught us to frame problems using user insights and map them to AI/ML capabilities. I mapped this to a conversational AI system that uses carefully constrained system prompts to avoid hallucination — the same principle I apply when designing LangGraph agent chains with explicit tool-calling boundaries.

Phase 3 — Ideate: I brainstormed multiple approaches: a rule-based FAQ bot (too rigid), a fine-tuned medical LLM (too expensive for a classroom exercise), and a system-prompt-engineered chatbot with retrieval grounding (most practical). The third option mirrors the RAG architecture I have built professionally — where the LLM's behaviour is shaped by a well-crafted system prompt plus retrieved context from a knowledge base. For this lab, I used system prompt engineering as the primary control mechanism.

Process

I followed a structured two-part approach to get the most out of the AI coaching session:

Part A — Foundational Concepts Explored
📈

Supervised Learning

The model learns from labelled data (input + correct output). It makes a guess, checks the answer, and adjusts its internal maths to reduce error each time. Like a student learning with an answer key.

🔎

Unsupervised Learning

No labels are given. The model must find hidden patterns, structures, or groupings on its own. Like sorting a pile of unlabelled photographs into albums by visual similarity.

🎮

Reinforcement Learning

The agent learns by trial and error, receiving rewards for good actions and penalties for bad ones. It figures out the best strategy (policy) to maximise total reward over time.

AI Coach Interaction — Key Concept Discussions
supervised-learning.chat
👤
Bhargav
How does a supervised learning model actually learn from training data? Walk me through the mechanics.
🤖
ML Coach
A supervised model learns by looking at labelled data — inputs paired with the correct output. It makes a prediction, compares it to the actual answer, calculates the error, and adjusts its internal weights to minimise that error. With each pass (epoch), the model gets better at mapping inputs to outputs.
💡 My connection: At A5G Networks, I built supervised pipelines for 5G anomaly detection — same principle: labelled network events → train → detect anomalies in production.
unsupervised-learning.chat
👤
Bhargav
In unsupervised learning, there are no labels. So what is the main approach the model uses to learn?
🤖
ML Coach
Since there are no correct answers provided, the main approach is to find hidden structures, patterns, or similarities in the data. The model groups similar data points together (clustering) or reduces dimensions to reveal underlying relationships.
💡 My connection: At Stony Brook, I applied unsupervised clustering on genomic datasets to extract feature groupings from unlabelled biological sequences.
reinforcement-learning.chat
👤
Bhargav
How does a reinforcement learning agent decide which actions are best if nobody tells it the right answer?
🤖
ML Coach
The agent learns purely through trial and error. It takes an action in an environment, receives either a positive reward or a negative penalty, and over many episodes it learns which policy (sequence of actions) gives the maximum cumulative reward. The key challenge is balancing exploration (trying new things) versus exploitation (doing what already works).
💡 My connection: At Cloudphysician, our ICU monitoring system used feedback-driven thresholds — similar explore/exploit logic — to alert doctors when patient vitals crossed critical boundaries.
training-fundamentals.chat
👤
Bhargav
Why do we need to pass the same data multiple times through the model? Why not just learn it in one go?
🤖
ML Coach
Iterating (running multiple epochs) helps the model slowly update its weights and fine-tune its understanding. A single pass would be like reading a textbook once before an exam — repetition helps the model generalise rather than memorise. Too few epochs lead to underfitting; too many lead to overfitting.
💡 My connection: I track training epochs, learning rate schedules, and early stopping with MLflow at A5G — knowing when to stop iterating is as important as starting.

Exploration vs. Exploitation in Reinforcement Learning
